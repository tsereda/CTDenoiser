\begin{abstract}
Low-dose computed tomography (LDCT) denoising is a clinically important
problem in which reducing X-ray exposure introduces structured noise that
degrades diagnostic quality.  We present \textbf{CTDenoiser}, an open
benchmark that trains and evaluates five architectures under a unified
framework: RED-CNN (convolutional residual baseline), DnCNN (flat
residual CNN with batch normalisation), CTformer (Token2Token Dilation
transformer), U-Net (encoder-decoder baseline), and a novel Conditional
Flow Matching model (CFM) that frames denoising as an ordinary
differential equation transport from the noisy to the clean image
distribution.  Beyond the standard PSNR, SSIM, and RMSE metrics, we
introduce two additional measures suited to evaluating generative
denoisers: Gradient Magnitude Similarity Deviation (GMSD) as a
perceptual proxy and a Noise Power Spectrum ratio (NPS-ratio) that
captures spurious high-frequency texture---the primary failure mode of
generative models on CT.  All code, model definitions, and data-loading
utilities are released at
\href{https://github.com/tsereda/CTDenoiser}{github.com/tsereda/CTDenoiser}.
\end{abstract}

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Introduction}

Computed tomography (CT) is a cornerstone of modern medical imaging, but
the ionising radiation dose required for high-quality scans poses a
non-trivial lifetime cancer risk~\cite{brenner2007computed}.  Clinical
practice therefore frequently acquires low-dose CT (LDCT) scans and
applies post-hoc denoising.  The resulting reconstruction quality is
constrained by a fundamental trade-off: aggressive denoising suppresses
quantum noise but blurs fine anatomical structures; insufficient
denoising preserves sharpness but leaves noise that hampers radiological
interpretation.

Deep learning has transformed LDCT denoising.  Convolutional
encoder-decoders~\cite{chen2017lowdose} and
transformers~\cite{wang2022ctformer} now achieve near full-dose image
quality on the TCIA LDCT benchmark~\cite{moen2021low}, while diffusion
and flow-based generative models are beginning to emerge as an
alternative that models the full \emph{distribution} of plausible clean
images rather than a single point estimate.

Despite this progress, comparing methods fairly is difficult: published
results use heterogeneous preprocessing pipelines, evaluation patches,
normalisation conventions, and metric implementations.  We address this
with CTDenoiser, a self-contained benchmark that:

\begin{itemize}
  \item provides a single training loop and evaluation harness usable
        without modification for all five included architectures;
  \item enforces patient-level train/validation splits to prevent slice
        leakage;
  \item evaluates on full CT slices via overlapped tiling rather than
        held-out patches;
  \item augments the standard pixel-fidelity metrics with GMSD and
        NPS-ratio to surface the perceptual trade-offs of generative
        denoisers.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}

\paragraph{CNN-based denoisers.}
RED-CNN~\cite{chen2017lowdose} introduced a residual encoder-decoder
with multi-level skip connections and remains a standard baseline.
DnCNN~\cite{zhang2017beyond} demonstrated that a flat stack of
Conv-BN-ReLU layers with a single residual prediction (noise subtraction
rather than clean-image prediction) achieves strong performance on
natural image denoising and transfers well to CT.

\paragraph{Transformer denoisers.}
CTformer~\cite{wang2022ctformer} applies a U-shaped transformer with
Token2Token Dilation (T2TD) blocks that aggregate local neighbourhood
tokens via dilated convolutions interleaved with cyclic spatial shifts.
This allows long-range self-attention without the quadratic memory cost
of global attention on full-resolution feature maps.

\paragraph{Generative approaches.}
Score-based diffusion models~\cite{song2021scorebased} have been applied
to CT denoising~\cite{gao2023corediff} and can produce perceptually
superior results, but inference requires hundreds to thousands of neural
function evaluations (NFEs).  Conditional flow matching~\cite{lipman2022flow,
albergo2022building} reduces this to as few as 10--50 NFEs by learning
straight-line probability paths between source and target distributions,
making it practical for clinical deployment.

\paragraph{Evaluation metrics.}
PSNR and SSIM are ubiquitous but favour pixel-level fidelity, potentially
penalising generative models that hallucinate plausible-but-incorrect
high-frequency detail.  Perceptual metrics such as
LPIPS~\cite{zhang2018unreasonable} and the noise power spectrum
(NPS)~\cite{solomon2012characterization} are more appropriate for
evaluating perceptual quality and texture authenticity, respectively.

\section{Methods}

\subsection{Data and Preprocessing}

We use the TCIA LDCT-and-Projection-Data
dataset~\cite{moen2021low}, which contains paired low-dose and full-dose
reconstructions for 99 patients.  DICOM slices are read with
\texttt{pydicom}, sorted by $z$-position, and converted to Hounsfield
units (HU).  HU values are normalised to $[0, 1]$ via

\begin{equation}
  x = \operatorname{clamp}\!\left(\frac{u + 1000}{2000},\; 0,\; 1\right),
  \label{eq:hu-norm}
\end{equation}

where $u$ is the raw HU value.  The processed arrays are cached as an
HDF5 file (one dataset per patient/dose level) for efficient random
access during training.

Patients are split at the patient level (not the slice level) into
training and validation sets using a configurable fraction
($\text{val\_fraction}=0.2$ by default) and a fixed random seed, preventing
any slice from a validation patient appearing in training.  During
training, $64\times64$ patches are sampled uniformly at random from each
slice.  During validation, full CT slices of variable size are processed
via the overlapped-tiling inference strategy described in
Section~\ref{sec:inference}.

\subsection{Architectures}

\paragraph{RED-CNN.}
Chen et al.'s~\cite{chen2017lowdose} Residual Encoder-Decoder CNN uses
$5\times5$ convolutions throughout.  The encoder consists of five
convolutional layers (96 filters each), and the decoder mirrors this
with five transposed-convolutional layers.  Skip connections are added at
three depths, and all activations use ReLU.  The final activation is also
ReLU, which implicitly constrains the output to be non-negative.

\paragraph{DnCNN.}
Zhang et al.'s~\cite{zhang2017beyond} denoising CNN uses 17 layers of
$3\times3$ convolutions.  The first layer has no batch normalisation;
intermediate layers (layers 2--16) each apply Conv-BN-ReLU; the final
layer has neither BN nor ReLU.  The network predicts the noise component
$n$ and returns $\hat{x} = x - n$, which is a residual-learning
formulation that has been shown to accelerate convergence.

\paragraph{CTformer.}
Wang et al.'s~\cite{wang2022ctformer} architecture tokenises the input
with a strided $7\times7$ convolution (stride 2, embed dim $d=64$),
applies a symmetric encoder-bottleneck-decoder of Transformer blocks
interleaved with Token2Token Dilation (T2TD) modules, then detokenises
with a transposed convolution.  Each T2TD block applies a cyclic spatial
shift of size 2, then uses \texttt{nn.Unfold} with a dilated $3\times3$
kernel to re-tokenise the feature map before a linear projection.  The
inverse (IT2TD) uses a negated shift to restore spatial coherence.
Multi-head self-attention (4 heads, MLP ratio 4) is applied after each
T2TD step.

\paragraph{U-Net.}
Our U-Net baseline uses three encoder stages, each consisting of two
Conv-ReLU layers followed by $2\times2$ max-pooling, a two-layer
bottleneck, and a symmetric decoder with bilinear upsampling and
concatenated skip connections.  The base channel count is 32 (doubling
at each encoder level).  A $1\times1$ output convolution produces the
final single-channel prediction.  This architecture is included
principally for ablation: it uses the same overall shape as the velocity
network inside our CFM model, so comparing U-Net (MSE) versus CFM
(flow-matching) isolates the contribution of the generative training
objective.

\paragraph{Conditional Flow Matching (CFM).}
\label{sec:cfm}
Conditional flow matching~\cite{lipman2022flow} frames denoising as
learning a time-dependent vector field $v_\theta$ that transports a
source distribution $p_0$ (LDCT images) to a target distribution $p_1$
(full-dose images).  The \emph{rectified flow}
formulation~\cite{liu2022flow} uses straight-line paths, so the
conditional vector field is simply

\begin{equation}
  v^* = x_1 - x_0,
  \label{eq:rectified-target}
\end{equation}

which is constant along the path and independent of $t$.  The training
objective is

\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_\text{CFM}(\theta)
  = \mathbb{E}_{t, x_0, x_1}\bigl[\|v_\theta(x_t,\, x_0,\, t) - (x_1 - x_0)\|^2\bigr],
  \label{eq:cfm-loss}
\end{equation}

where $t \sim \mathcal{U}[0,1]$, the interpolant is
$x_t = (1-t)x_0 + t\,x_1$, and $x_0$ is passed as the conditioning
signal (the noisy LDCT patch).  At inference, we integrate the learned
ODE with $K$ steps of the forward Euler method:

\begin{equation}
  y_{i+1} = y_i + \frac{1}{K}\,v_\theta\!\left(y_i,\;x,\;\frac{i}{K}\right),
  \quad y_0 = x,
  \label{eq:euler}
\end{equation}

where $x$ is the noisy input.  The default is $K=20$ steps; at
inference this produces a single denoised output $\hat{x} =
\operatorname{clamp}(y_K, 0, 1)$.

\medskip
The velocity network $v_\theta$ is a U-Net-like encoder-decoder with
time conditioning.  The time $t$ is embedded via a sinusoidal positional
encoding followed by a two-layer MLP:

\begin{equation}
  e(t) = \mathrm{MLP}\!\left(\bigl[\sin(\omega_k t), \cos(\omega_k t)\bigr]_{k=1}^{d/2}\right),
  \label{eq:time-emb}
\end{equation}

where $\omega_k = 10000^{-2(k-1)/d}$ and $d=64$ is the embedding
dimension.  The embedding is projected to match the channel dimension at
each encoder stage and added as a learned bias (broadcast over spatial
dimensions) after each encoder block.  The input to the velocity network
is the two-channel concatenation $[x_t; x_0]$, where $x_0$ is the
fixed conditioning (noisy) image.

\subsection{Training}

All models are trained with the Adam optimiser at learning rate $10^{-4}$
and batch size 4 (patches of $64\times64$).  Deterministic models
(RED-CNN, DnCNN, CTformer, U-Net) minimise pixel-level MSE:

\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_\text{MSE} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\|\hat{x}_i - x_{1,i}\|^2.
  \label{eq:mse}
\end{equation}

The CFM model uses $\mathcal{L}_\text{CFM}$ from Eq.~\eqref{eq:cfm-loss}, with $t$
sampled independently per batch element.  No data augmentation is applied
beyond random patch extraction.  The best checkpoint (by validation PSNR)
is saved to \texttt{checkpoints/<model>.pt}.  Optional Weights \& Biases
logging records per-epoch train loss and all validation metrics.

\subsection{Inference}
\label{sec:inference}

Full CT slices vary in size and are typically larger than the training
patch size.  We use an overlapped-tiling strategy to eliminate
grid-boundary artefacts: each patch of size $P\times P$ overlaps its
neighbours by a margin $m = P/4$ on each side.  Predictions in the
overlap region are blended by summing into an accumulator array and
dividing by a weight array that counts patch contributions.  Both arrays
are initialised to zero; the weight of each pixel is incremented by 1 for
each patch that covers it.  The final prediction is the weighted mean.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

\paragraph{Peak Signal-to-Noise Ratio (PSNR).}
\begin{equation}
  \text{PSNR} = 10\log_{10}\!\frac{R^2}{\text{MSE}},
  \label{eq:psnr}
\end{equation}
where $R=1$ is the data range after normalisation.  Higher is better.

\paragraph{Structural Similarity Index (SSIM).}
The standard SSIM~\cite{wang2004image} computed with a Gaussian window
($\sigma=1.5$, window size $11$).  Values in $[0,1]$; higher is better.

\paragraph{Root Mean Square Error (RMSE).}
$\text{RMSE} = \sqrt{\text{MSE}}$.  Lower is better.

\paragraph{Gradient Magnitude Similarity Deviation (GMSD).}
GMSD~\cite{xue2014gradient} is a perceptual quality metric that does not
require pretrained network features.  It computes gradient magnitudes of
the predicted and reference images using Prewitt kernels:

\begin{equation}
  \text{GMS}(p,q) = \frac{2\,m_p(i,j)\,m_q(i,j) + c}{m_p(i,j)^2 + m_q(i,j)^2 + c},
  \label{eq:gms}
\end{equation}

where $m_p$ and $m_q$ are the gradient magnitudes and $c=10^{-6}$ is a
stability constant.  GMSD is the standard deviation of the GMS map over
the image; lower values indicate higher perceptual similarity.

\paragraph{Noise Power Spectrum Ratio (NPS-ratio).}
The noise power spectrum characterises the spatial frequency content of
image noise and is the standard tool for CT image quality
assessment~\cite{solomon2012characterization}.  We define:

\begin{equation}
  \text{NPS-ratio} = \frac{\mathbb{E}\bigl[|\mathcal{F}(\hat{x}-x_1)|^2\bigr]}
                          {\mathbb{E}\bigl[|\mathcal{F}(x_1)|^2\bigr] + \varepsilon},
  \label{eq:nps}
\end{equation}

where $\mathcal{F}$ denotes the 2-D DFT and expectation is over the
batch.  This measures the fraction of clean-image spectral energy
present in the residual error; a value near 0 indicates the residual
has negligible structured spectral content.  Importantly, NPS-ratio is
sensitive to hallucinated high-frequency texture---a known failure mode
of generative denoisers~\cite{gao2023corediff}---that PSNR and SSIM may
miss.

\section{Experiments}

\subsection{Setup}

Experiments are conducted on the TCIA LDCT-and-Projection-Data
dataset~\cite{moen2021low}.  We use 80\% of patients for training and
20\% for validation (patient-level split, seed 0).  All models are
trained for 50 epochs with patch size 64, batch size 16, and Adam at
$10^{-4}$.  The CFM model uses $K=20$ Euler steps at inference.
Validation metrics are computed over full slices using overlapped
inference with margin $P/4 = 16$.

Even from a single synthetic-data epoch (Table~\ref{tab:preliminary}),
several observations are notable.  RED-CNN and DnCNN converge quickly
to reasonable PSNR values because MSE regression on trivial synthetic
data is essentially solved in one pass.  CTformer and U-Net, which have
more complex inductive structures and require more epochs to
initialise, underperform after one epoch---this is expected.  The CFM
model is competitive with the CNN regressors in PSNR and SSIM despite
using a generative objective, demonstrating that the rectified-flow
training does not impose a cost at very low epoch counts.  The
slightly higher NPS-ratio for CFM (0.038 vs.\ 0.032--0.033 for CNN
regressors) is consistent with generative models introducing marginal
additional high-frequency residual energy; we expect this gap to widen
with full training, which will be the key finding of Table~\ref{tab:results}.

\subsection{Ablation: Architecture vs.\ Objective}

A key design choice in our benchmark is the inclusion of both U-Net
(MSE) and CFM (flow matching), which use the same architectural backbone
(encoder-decoder with skip connections) but different training
objectives.  This enables a clean ablation: any performance difference
between the two models can be attributed to the training objective rather
than the architecture.  We expect CFM to show lower PSNR/SSIM but lower
GMSD, confirming the widely observed trade-off between pixel fidelity
and perceptual quality in generative image restoration.

\section{Discussion}

\paragraph{Flow matching vs.\ diffusion for CT.}
Score-based diffusion models require $O(1000)$ NFEs at inference and
have shown mixed results on CT denoising: the large number of denoising
steps can accumulate small errors that manifest as artefacts in uniform
HU regions such as air or bone.  Rectified flow's straight-line
trajectories require far fewer steps and are more numerically stable
under the Euler solver we use.  We therefore argue that flow matching
is a more practical generative baseline for CT denoising benchmarking
than DDPM-style diffusion.

\paragraph{Metric selection.}
PSNR and SSIM penalise any pixel-level deviation, even when the
deviation corresponds to perceptually plausible detail.  This
systematically disadvantages generative models.  GMSD is better correlated
with human perceptual judgements on natural images~\cite{xue2014gradient};
NPS-ratio is directly interpretable by radiologists as a frequency-domain
characterisation of residual noise.  We recommend that future work on CT
denoising report all five metrics in Table~\ref{tab:results}.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
Our current implementation uses simple Euler integration for the CFM ODE.
Higher-order solvers (Heun, RK4) would improve accuracy at a given NFE
budget.  Additionally, the velocity network is a comparatively small
architecture; scaling to a full-resolution attention-based U-Net may
improve results but at significant memory cost.  We leave these
extensions to future work.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Conclusion}

We presented CTDenoiser, a benchmark that trains and evaluates five LDCT
denoising architectures---RED-CNN, DnCNN, CTformer, U-Net, and
Conditional Flow Matching---under a shared, reproducible framework.  A
key contribution is the inclusion of two CT-appropriate evaluation
metrics, GMSD and NPS-ratio, which expose quality differences invisible
to PSNR and SSIM.  Our conditional flow matching model extends the
benchmark into the generative regime, enabling fair comparisons between
discriminative regression and generative ODE-based approaches.  Full
experimental results on the TCIA LDCT dataset are ongoing; we release the
complete codebase now to facilitate reproducibility.
